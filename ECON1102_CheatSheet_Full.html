GDP: DEFINITION & MEASUREMENT
GDP = monetary value of FINAL goods & services
produced in a country during a given period.
Monetary ($), final only (no intermediates = double counting), produced (not bought), flow (quarterly).
EXCLUDED:
Financial txns (transfers, share sales)
2nd-hand goods (no new production)
Unpaid housework (no market price)
INCLUDED (unpriced):
Govt services: valued at COST of provision
Owner-occ rent: IMPUTED by ABS from census data
3 Methods + GNIEXPENDITURE: Y = C+I+G+X-M INCOME: GDP = WL+RK+Net Indirect Tax PRODUCTION: sum of Value Added VA = Sales - Cost of Intermediates GNI = GDP + Net Primary Income
C=consump I=invest G=govt X=exp M=imp
GDP=location; GNI=nationality of income
Sum VA across all firms = GDP (no double count)
Y+M = C+I+G+X: supply of G&S = demand for G&S
NOMINAL vs REAL GDP
Nominal: current-yr prices (inc inflation effect).
Real: base-yr prices (volume only). Real = what affects living standards (quantity not price).
Core RelationshipsNom GDP = Price Level x Real GDP Real GDP = Nom GDP / Price Level Price Lvl = Nom GDP / Real GDP GDP Deflator = Nom GDP / Real GDP %dNom ~ %dPrices + %dReal Real GDP p.c. = Real GDP / Pop %dReal p.c. ~ %dReal GDP - %dPop
Chain-weighting:
Calc growth using BOTH Laspeyres (initial prices) AND Paasche (final prices), then AVERAGE them.
Apply year-by-year for long series. Avoids bias from choice of base year over long periods.
Chain ExampleLasp=4.5%, Paasche=5.6%
Chain=(4.5+5.6)/2 = 5.05%
If base yr 2007, Real GDP 2007=201,000:
Real GDP 2008 = 201,000 x 1.0505 = 211,151
Business Cycles:
Recession: >=2 consec qtrs neg real GDP growth.
Peak=high before contraction. Trough=low before recovery. Expansion=GDP rising.
Aus last recession: H1 2020 (COVID). Before that: early 1990s.

CPI, DEFLATOR & INFLATION
Price FormulasCPI(t)=[Cost basket(t)/Cost basket(base)]x100 pi(t)=[CPI(t)-CPI(t-1)]/CPI(t-1) x 100 GDP Deflator = Nom GDP / Real GDP
CPI Worked ExamplesBase=$680, Now=$850. CPI=850/680=1.25
pi=(1.25-1)/1 x100 = 25%
If CPI yr1=105, yr2=108:
pi=(108-105)/105 x100 = 2.86%

CPI vs Deflator: CPI uses FIXED basket; deflator uses current production weights. CPI measures cost of living; deflator measures price of all GDP.
INFLATION: BIASES & COSTS
CPI biases (all OVERSTATE true inflation):
Quality: improvements look like price rises
New goods: excluded from basket until rebase
Substitution: fixed basket ignores consumers
  switching to cheaper alternatives.
Costs of UNEXPECTED inflation:
Redistributes: borrowers gain, lenders lose
Hurts fixed nominal income earners
Distorts tax system (if not indexed)
Noise in relative price signals (can't tell
  individual price change vs general level).
Costs of ANTICIPATED inflation:
Shoe-leather: frequent bank trips (money
  loses purchasing power over time).
Menu costs: real resources spent changing prices.
Optimal inflation: 1-3% p.a.
Avoids deflation (discourages spending/invest)
Allows moderate falls in real wages (stim LD)
Promotes demand for goods & services
Most countries have explicit inflation targets.
LABOUR MARKET DEFINITIONS
Employed: >=1hr work/wk for pay (or on leave).
During COVID: attached to employer (on JobKeeper).
Unemployed: not working + actively looked in past month + available to start (or waiting on new job).
Labour Force: LF = L + U.
Pop = working-age (15+, civilian, usually resident).
NOT in LF: homeworkers, volunteers, disabled, voluntarily inactive, DISCOURAGED workers.
Key Ratiosu = (U/LF) x 100 [unemp rate] PR = (LF/Pop) x 100 [participation] E/P = (L/Pop) x 100 [emp-to-pop]

Discouraged: given up search despite wanting work, believe no suitable jobs avail. Counted as NLF.
Full-time: >=35hrs/wk. Casual: no paid leave.
TYPES OF UNEMPLOYMENT
FRICTIONAL (short-term):
Job search between positions. Normal & BENEFICIAL (better worker-job matching). Any dynamic economy.
STRUCTURAL (long-term):
Skills mismatch. Causes: automation, offshoring, structural change, lack of skills, discrimination.
CYCLICAL (variable):
Business cycle. Rises recessions, falls booms.
Natural RatedU = s*L - f*U Steady state (dU=0): u(fric) = s/(s+f) x 100 u* = u(fric)+u(struct) [natural rate] Cyclical unemp = u - u* u*>0 always (even at 'full employment')
s=separation rate, f=job-finding rate
Policies can influence s (employment services)
or f (training programs, job matching)
Frictional Unemp ExampleIf s=0.02, f=0.18:
u(fric)=0.02/(0.02+0.18)x100 = 10%
If s=0.01, f=0.19:
u(fric)=0.01/0.20 x100 = 5%
Lower s or higher f -> lower u(fric).
OUTPUT GAPS & OKUN'S LAW
Y* = potential output:
GDP when L and K at NORMAL utilisation rates. NOT maximum. Grows over time with labour force, capital stock, technology.
Y not always = Y*.
Output GapOutput Gap = Y - Y* Output Gap(%) = (Y-Y*)/Y* x 100 POSITIVE (Y>Y*): EXPANSIONARY Firms above normal capacity -> raise prices -> INFLATIONARY NEGATIVE (Y high unemployment Both gaps are problems for policymakers.
Okun's LawOKUN'S LAW: (Y-Y*)/Y* x 100 = -B(u - u*) B ~ 2 for Australia (RBA: ~1.25) 1pp rise cyclical unemp -> B% fall in gap Negative sign: u up -> gap down (inverse)
Okun Worked ExamplesEx1: u:5%->7%, u*=5%, B=2
  Cyclical=2pp. Gap=-2x2=-4% [below Y*]
Ex2: u:4%->3%, u*=4%, B=2
  Cyclical=-1pp. Gap=-2x(-1)=+2% [above Y*]
  Second case: INFLATIONARY pressure.
Ex3: u=u*=5%: Gap=-2x(5-5)=0% [at potential]
LABOUR DEMAND & SUPPLY
MPL = extra output from 1 more worker (K fixed). DIMINISHING returns. VMPL = p x MPL.
Labour Demand & SupplyFirm: VMPL = p x MPL = W [nominal] Equiv: MPL = W/P [real wage] Hire workers until VMPL = wage. If MPL > W/P: hire more (profitable). If MPL < W/P: too many, lay off. LD shifts R: higher p or higher MPL LS shifts R: higher Pop or higher PR LS = increasing fn of real wage (W/P)
Higher productivity effect:
LD shifts right -> move along LS -> BOTH real wage AND employment increase.
Frictions -> involuntary unemployment:
Min wage above equil: Ls>Ld=unemployment
  Those employed (Ld) better off at higher W.
  Gap Ls-Ld = people who WANT work but can't.
Unions: collective bargain -> W above clearing
Taxes: wedge between firm cost & take-home
Competitive model: no frictions = no involuntary unemp. Flexible W/P clears market always.

L=2->8, L=3->6, L=4->4, L=5->2.
W=$10/hr. W/P=10/2=5.

INTEREST RATES
Nominal i: return in MONEY terms.
Real r: return in GOODS (adjusted for inflation).
EXPECTED real rate matters for decisions.
Fisher Equationr = i - pi [ex-post, actual] r = i - pi(e) [ex-ante, expected] FISHER EFFECT: i = r + pi(e) Nominal rate moves 1-for-1 with expected inflation (r constant)
Fisher Effect ExamplesWant real return 5%:
  pi(e)=3%: i=5+3=8%
  pi(e)=1%: i=5+1=6%
  pi(e)=5%: i=5+5=10%
  pi(e)=-6%: i=5-6=-1% (problem!)
  Lose nominal money. Better: don't lend.
  -> ZLB: i>=0 historically.
  If i=0: r=-pi(e).
Japan/Europe: negative i observed.

WK3: KEYNESIAN FRAMEWORK
KEY: Prices STICKY in short run.
Firms vary PRODUCTION to meet demand (not prices).
SR: demand falls -> output+employment fall (not P).
SR: demand rises -> output+employment rise (not P).
LR: sustained demand changes -> price adjustment.
Micro: firms have market power + menu costs (real cost of changing prices). Not perfectly competitive.
Contrast: frictionless view = flexible P&W absorb demand shocks, no output/employment change.
PAE & EquilibriumPAE = C + I(P) + G + X - M I(P)=planned investment Actual: Y = C+I+G+X-M I = I(P) + dInv(unplanned) EQUILIBRIUM: Y = PAE <=> I=I(P) <=> dInv(unpl)=0 Y = PAE + unplanned inventory change
Disequilibrium adjustment:
Y>PAE: inventories pile up (goods producers)
  or excess capacity (service). CUT output -> Y dn.
Y<PAE: inventories deplete (goods) or
  insufficient capacity (service). RAISE output -> Y up.
Continues until Y=PAE at equilibrium.
CONSUMPTION FUNCTION
Keynesian ConsumptionC = C(0) + c(Y - T) C(0) = autonomous consumption (wealth, confidence, interest rates; determined OUTSIDE model) c = MPC (0 < c < 1) $1 rise (Y-T) -> $c rise in C (less than $1; rest is SAVED) MPC = dC/d(Y-T) = c APC = C/(Y-T) = C(0)/(Y-T) + c APC > MPC; APC -> MPC as Y rises (C(0)/(Y-T) shrinks toward 0)
Saving Function (Derived)S = Y-C = -C(0) + (1-c)Y [saving fn] MPS = 1-c MPC+MPS = 1 c=0.75: MPS=0.25 $1 extra: 75c spent, 25c saved
45-deg line: y=x (slope=1). All Y=PAE points.
Equil: PAE line (slope=c<1) crosses 45-deg line.
Slope < 1 guarantees unique crossing = unique equil.
C(0) shifts PAE line UP/DOWN (parallel shift).
c changes SLOPE of PAE line.

2-SECTOR MODEL & MULTIPLIER
No govt (G=T=0), closed economy (X=M=0).
Full DerivationPAE = C(0) + cY + I(0) Set Y=PAE: Y = C(0)+cY+I(0) Y-cY = C(0)+I(0) Y(1-c) = C(0)+I(0) Y(e) = [1/(1-c)] x (C(0)+I(0)) MULTIPLIER = 1/(1-c) > 1 dY/dC(0) = dY/dI(0) = 1/(1-c)
Multiplier Tablec=0.75: M=1/0.25=4.  $100->dY=$400
c=0.80: M=1/0.20=5.  $100->dY=$500
c=0.50: M=1/0.50=2.  $100->dY=$200
c=0.90: M=1/0.10=10. $50->dY=$500
Round-by-round process:
dPAE=100 -> $100 income -> spend c*100 -> new income c*100 -> spend c^2*100 -> ... converges.
Total: 100x[1+c+c^2+...] = 100/(1-c).
Works because 0<c<1 so geometric series converges.
Round-by-Round Examplec=0.8, dI(0)=100. Rounds:
R1: dY=100, R2: dY=80, R3: dY=64,
R4: dY=51.2, R5: dY=41.0, ...
Total: 100/0.2 = 500.

BALANCED BUDGET MULTIPLIER
Raise G(0) and T(0) by SAME amount.
Initial BB unchanged: (T+100)-(G+100)=T-G.
BBM DerivationSet dG(0)=dT(0), dC(0)=dI(0)=0: dY=[1/(1-c(1-t))] x [-c*dG(0)+dG(0)] =[1/(1-c(1-t))] x (1-c) x dG(0) BBM = (1-c)/[1-c(1-t)] POSITIVE but LESS THAN 1
BBM Worked Examplec=0.6,t=0.3: BBM=0.4/0.58=0.69
$100 balanced-budget -> dY=$69

Intuition: +$100G fully enters PAE.
But +$100T reduces C by c*100=$60 only
(HH save $40). Net on PAE = +$40.
Amplified by mult: 0.4 x 1.72 = 0.69.
AUTOMATIC STABILISERS
T=T(0)+tY auto-dampens GDP fluctuations:
GDP dn -> tax rev dn, transfers (eg unemp
  benefits) rise -> Y(D) cushioned -> smaller C fall.
GDP up -> tax rev up, transfers fall
  -> restrains overheating.
NO govt action. BB auto-moves to deficit in recessions, surplus in booms.
Also: t>0 makes multiplier SMALLER than t=0: 1/[1-c(1-t)] < 1/(1-c). Tax auto-leaks each round of spending, reducing amplification of shocks.
DISCRETIONARY FISCAL POLICY
Deliberate changes in G, T(0), TR, or t.
TIME LAGS (why fiscal < monetary):
Recognition: data lags to detect state of econ
Decision: choose appropriate policy response
Implementation: needs legislation/parliament
Effect: time for policy to influence economy
Policy should be FORWARD-LOOKING (forecasts).
Prior to GFC, fiscal seen as less important than monetary. GFC/COVID revived fiscal activism.
To close NEGATIVE gap (Y<Y*):
Increase G -> PAE up -> Y(e) rises toward Y*
Decrease T(0) -> Y(D) up -> C up -> PAE up
To close POSITIVE gap (Y>Y*):
Decrease G or Increase T(0).
PUBLIC DEBT & SUSTAINABILITY
Debt FormulasBB=T-G. D(t)=D(t-1)-BB(t) Deficit(BB<0)->debt up. Surplus->debt dn. BUDGET CONSTRAINT: G(t)+TR(t)+r*D(t-1)=TA(t)+[D(t)-D(t-1)] [uses of funds] [sources of funds] r*D(t-1)=interest on outstanding debt D(t)-D(t-1)=new borrowing DEBT-TO-GDP DYNAMICS: dd(t)=[(r-g)/(1+g)]*d(t-1) - pbb(t) d=D/Y, g=real GDP growth pbb=PBB/Y, PBB=TA-G-TR (excl interest) 3 sources: taxes, borrowing, money.
d INCREASES when:
Primary budget deficits (pbb<0)
r>g (interest exceeds GDP growth)
Fiscal rules:
Balance budget over business cycle (deficit
  in recession, surplus in expansion).
Golden rule: current exp from taxes; capital
  invest from borrowing (pay-as-you-use; future generations benefit & share cost via taxes).
Costs of high debt:
Lower growth (empirical evidence)
Crowding out (deficit->r up->private I dn
  ->lower K->lower LR growth).
Intergenerational inequity (current gen
  benefits, future bears tax burden).
Money financing -> hyperinflation (pi>50%/mo)
  Avoided by modern advanced economies.
  Zimbabwe 2000s, Venezuela = examples.
Aus: d currently stable; (r-g)~0, pbb~0.
